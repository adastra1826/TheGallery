ROOM I · SPECIMEN CS.011

Ultramarine

the blue from beyond the sea

Ultramarinus — the blue that came from beyond the sea, and for four centuries cost more than the gold it was asked to outshine.

Every grain in this drawer began in one mountainside. The lapis mines of Sar-e-Sang hang above the Kokcha river in Badakhshan, in the far northeast of Afghanistan, and they have been worked for over six thousand years — miners set fires against the rock face and quenched the hot stone with water until it split. Lapis went west to Egypt as beads and amulets and the inlaid brows of Tutankhamun's mask. But that was stone. The secret of turning it into paint took another four millennia.

Grind lapis and you get grey — the blue lazurite is locked in with calcite and flecks of pyrite. The trick, written down by Cennino Cennini around 1400, is to knead the powder into a dough of wax, pine resin and gum mastic, then work the dough in lye. The blue releases first; the grey stays behind. The first kneading yields the finest grade, and each pass after it something paler, down to the ash the workshop swept.

At that price the color was contractual. Venetian market grades ran to eight ducats the ounce — above gold — and patrons wrote the blue into altarpiece commissions by grade and by placement: this mantle, that heaven, nothing else. Michelangelo's Entombment sits unfinished in the National Gallery with a bare patch where the Virgin's robe waited on blue. Vermeer never waited — he ground it into shadows, wall plaster, the turban of a girl with a pearl earring — and left his widow bankrupt for it.

The monopoly fell to chemistry. In 1824 the Société d'Encouragement offered six thousand francs for a synthetic route; by 1826 Jean-Baptiste Guimet of Toulouse had one, and his ultramarine sold at roughly a four-hundredth the price of the stone. The sea kept the name. It gave up everything else.

CONSERVATORGrade I powder. Pyrite flecks visible at 10× — do not test with acid; see sickness file, 1972.

LOAN HISTORYRequested 1994, 2011. Refused both times. The blue does not travel.

“A color illustrious, beautiful, and most perfect, beyond all other colors.”

BENCH NOTES

The chromophore is no metal but a trapped radical: S3− anions caged in a sodalite lattice, Na8Al6Si6O24S4. Lightfast for centuries; ruined in minutes by acid — the pallor conservators call ultramarine sickness. The raw stone carries pyrite, which is why this mound glitters faintly gold.

ALONG THE CORRIDOR ROOM II · VERMILION

ROOM II · SPECIMEN CS.002

Vermilion

the red that Rome fixed by law

Rome wanted this red so badly it capped the price by statute and staffed the mine with the condemned.

Cinnabar came to Rome from Almadén in Castile, the richest mercury deposit on earth, worked by convicts under the sentence damnatio ad metalla — condemned to the mines, a sentence whose brevity was the point. Pliny records the state fixing vermilion at seventy sesterces the pound so speculators could not corner it. Rome spent it anyway: the great frieze of the Villa of the Mysteries at Pompeii is drenched in a red that still radiates from the wall two thousand years on.

China knew the color earlier and kept it longer. Shang diviners rubbed cinnabar into oracle-bone inscriptions; Sima Qian wrote that the tomb of the First Emperor held rivers of mercury; the carved lacquer of the Yongle workshops built vermilion up in two hundred coats and cut landscapes into the thickness. And it was Chinese and Arab alchemists who learned, by the eighth century, to make the red without the mine — mercury and sulfur sealed in a vessel and heated until they sublime into synthetic cinnabar. A thousand years before the word existed, vermilion was already a triumph of chemistry.

Medieval scriptoria poured it into initial letters and headings — the rubrica, red earth, that gives us “rubric” and the red-letter day. When Titian raised the Virgin off the ground in the Assunta, he did it in vermilion robes against a golden sky: the loudest red in the loudest room in Venice.

Its flaw is a slow betrayal. Under light and trace halides, red α-HgS converts to black metacinnabar — grain by grain, the color eats itself. The reds of Pompeii have been darkening since the day they were dug out of the ash. The drawer note reads: keep dark; the specimen is doing what the frescoes did.

CONDITIONDarkening at drawer-front edge; metacinnabar suspected. Specimen rotated 03/2024.

HANDLINGMercury. Weigh in the hood; log to the mercury register, not the day book.

“Sound vermilion is made only in the fire; the friar who taught me kept the vessel sealed and prayed the color through it.”

BENCH NOTES

HgS. Refractive index 3.1 — few substances carry more light per grain, which is why a thin film of it covers so completely. Photosensitive: red α-cinnabar inverts to black metacinnabar where chlorides ride in with the binder. Grind it coarse and it cools; fine, and it flares orange.

ALONG THE CORRIDOR ROOM III · TYRIAN PURPLE

ROOM III · SPECIMEN CS.017

Tyrian Purple

a quarter-million snails to the ounce

The smallest holding in the vault: less than half a gram, and the vault is rich to have it.

The purple comes from a gland the size of a rice grain in two Mediterranean sea snails, Bolinus brandaris and Hexaplex trunculus. Each yields a drop. The arithmetic never improves: a quarter of a million snails to one ounce of dye. The crushed-shell middens of Tyre and Sidon still stand as hills, and Strabo noted that the dye works made the city rich and unbearable in the same breath — the vats were banished downwind.

What the money bought was not brightness but permanence. Pliny called the best grade the color of clotted blood — dark, almost sullen — and unlike every plant dye it deepened with sun and weather instead of dying. Diocletian's price edict of 301 CE lists a pound of purple-dyed silk at one hundred fifty thousand denarii, against a farm laborer's twenty-five a day. Wearing it was legislation; counterfeiting it, treason.

Byzantium turned the color into constitutional law. Empresses were delivered in the Porphyra, a chamber walled in purple stone, so that heirs would be porphyrogénnētos — born in the purple; emperors signed in purple ink no other hand might use. When Constantinople fell in 1453 the vats went cold for good, and the church that had dressed in murex resorted to the scale insect: cardinals wear kermes red because the purple died with the city.

The molecule kept its secret until 1909, when Paul Friedländer took twelve thousand murex from the Trieste fish market and won 1.37 grams of pure dye — the assay that fixed the legend in decimal places. It proved to be indigo carrying two bromine atoms: ordinary blue, salted by the sea into empire.

SECURITYVial weighed monthly. Last check 0.4102 g — the missing milligrams are on the balance pan, 1963.

ALONG THE CORRIDOR · GLOVES ON ROOM IV · ORPIMENT

ROOM IV · SPECIMEN CS.023 · GLOVES REQUIRED

Orpiment

the gold that kills

Auripigmentum — “gold paint.” The one room in the vault where the lights seem to come from the drawer.

No yellow in the pre-modern world burned like it. Orpiment is arsenic trisulfide, dug in glittering, micaceous plates, and its Latin name says exactly what everyone hoped: gold pigment. Alchemists reasoned that a stone this color must hold gold the way ore holds metal, and spent centuries trying to talk it out. The stone held arsenic, and it never once lied about that.

Egypt used it where radiance mattered most: the yellows of the Book of the Dead of Ani, the painted goods of Tutankhamun's tomb, skin of gods rendered in a mineral that outshone ochre the way flame outshines brick. Persian and Byzantine illuminators laid it beside real gold leaf and let the two argue across the page.

Cennini entered the warning into the handbook itself: beware of soiling your mouth with it, lest you suffer personal injury. Venice ignored him — with lagoon light to answer, Titian and Veronese glazed orpiment over its orange twin realgar and got draperies that seem lit from inside the canvas. The workshops paid in the usual coin: shaking hands, ruined guts, short careers.

Even its chemistry is antisocial. Set beside lead- or copper-bearing colors it trades sulfur and blackens both parties, so it had to be laid alone, quarantined on the palette like the poison it is. Cadmium yellow finally retired it in the 1840s. The vault keeps its 188 grams double-sealed, and the room keeps the label short: handle with gloves; admire without them.

HAZARDArsenic. Gloves and mask; no open handling since 1977. The drawer liner is changed sealed.

INCOMPATIBLELead white, verdigris, all copper greens. Stored four cases from each.

ALONG THE CORRIDOR ROOM V · PRUSSIAN BLUE

ROOM V · SPECIMEN CS.031

Prussian Blue

the accident that made blue cheap

A colorman reached for the wrong potash and red came out blue. Every century since has been painted with the mistake.

Berlin, 1706. Johann Jacob Diesbach, renting bench space in the laboratory of the alchemist Johann Konrad Dippel, set out to precipitate a batch of cochineal red lake. The potash he borrowed was fouled with Dippel's animal oil — a distillate of bone and blood. The vat refused to go red. It went a deep, foreign blue that no European workshop had ever mixed: iron and cyanide locked in a lattice, the first synthetic pigment of the modern age.

The recipe was hoarded for nearly twenty years until John Woodward published it in the Philosophical Transactions of 1724, and then the color went everywhere at once. It sold for a tenth the price of ultramarine. For the first time in the history of painting, deep blue belonged to whoever wanted it — guild master or journeyman, Canaletto's skies or a shop sign in a market town.

It crossed to Japan in Dutch cargo through Nagasaki, where printmakers called it bero-ai, Berlin indigo. Hokusai took it and pulled a wave over Mount Fuji: the Great Wave is a Prussian-blue picture, and so is the whole late flowering of the ukiyo-e sky. It came back west in Van Gogh's night and Picasso's three blue years — the pigment of grief bought by the tube.

The vault's least precious specimen is the one the world still uses most. Its open lattice cages heavy atoms, so hospitals stock Prussian blue as the antidote for thallium and radioactive cesium; Herschel's cyanotype prints in it, which is why plans are blueprints. Two hundred grams, drawer nine — the cheapest room in the vault, and the one the modern world walked out of.

ACQUISITIONSReplaced freely; the vault buys it by the jar. The 1889 accession is kept for sentiment.

STORAGEKeep from lime and alkali. Otherwise the least demanding tenant on the floor.

“NB — the blue arises only when the potash has stood over the oil of bones. The error is the recipe.”

BENCH NOTES

Fe4[Fe(CN)6]3 — iron in two oxidation states, and the blue is the electron caught commuting between them. The lattice's cages swallow cesium and thallium whole, a pigment moonlighting as medicine. Weak against alkali: lime walls bleach it, so it never conquered fresco.
