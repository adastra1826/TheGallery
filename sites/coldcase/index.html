FILE 1974-C-0117 In the matter of the disappearance of
IRIS ANN CALLOWAY

Harlow Island, Washington  ·  Thursday, January 17, 1974
Reporting officer: Det. H. Quist, Harlow County Sheriff’s Office

UNRESOLVED
RECEIVEDNOV 3 1987COUNTY ARCHIVES
REVIEW 1994 no one came. —H.Q.

At 9:12 on the coldest night of that winter, Iris Calloway bought a one‑way ferry ticket to the mainland. The purser clips every ticket at the gangway. Hers was found two days later on the dock planking — unclipped. Twenty‑four tickets sold for the 9:40 sailing. Twenty‑three passengers counted aboard.

The board below is exactly as Detective Quist left it in 1985. Drag anything. Pull the string. Read everything twice.

THE NIGHT, RECONSTRUCTED

Hour by hour, from the last dinner to the cold kettle. Sources in brackets. Three entries never squared — Quist circled them in grease pencil.

  1. 18:10

    Iris arrives for Thursday dinner at her sister’s house on Marrow Road. She eats almost nothing and leaves before the pie. Frances asks what’s wrong. “The numbers at work aren’t right,” she says, “and they know I know.”

    [DOC‑07]
  2. 19:35

    The lamp goes on in the front room of the Spar Lane cottage. Mrs. Ostrander, across the lane, sees Iris at the window with papers in her hand.

    [DOC‑06]
  3. 20:05

    The party line rings: two longs and a short — the Calloway pattern. Mrs. Ostrander lifts her receiver, as one does. A man’s voice, low and even: “Bring the pages. We’ll square it tonight.” She hangs up, because it is none of her business.

    [DOC‑06]
  4. 20:55

    Porch light off. Iris walks toward the landing road, scarf over her hair, carrying a folder under one arm and nothing else. Her purse stays on its hook. The kettle stays on the ring.

    [DOC‑06, P‑4]
  5. 21:12

    Ticket window, Harlow Landing. One way to Ancoram, ticket No. 04471, forty cents. She asks agent Pearl Voss whether the 9:40 is running on time. It is.

    [E‑2]
  6. 21:15

    Walter Brandt settles his tab at the Anchor Room and leaves alone. The bartender remembers because Brandt, for the first time in nine years, did not finish the beer.

    [DOC‑09]
  7. 21:20

    Delbert Coyle, moving freight, watches Iris pass the freight shed heading north. The ferry slip is south. The cannery pier is north. There is nothing north in January.

    [DOC‑02]wrong direction. she wasn’t catching a boat. —H.Q.
  8. 21:30

    No witness. Only the matchbook on her telephone table, in her own hand: “W.B. — 9:30 — pier gate.”

    [E‑3]
  9. 21:40

    MV Tessandra departs Harlow Landing in light snow. Tickets sold for the sailing: twenty‑four. Passengers counted aboard by purser Holt: twenty‑three. Ticket No. 04471 is found Saturday on the dock planking — unclipped. Nobody boards this boat without a clipped ticket.

    [DOC‑03, E‑2]the ticket is the whole case. —H.Q.
  10. 22:05

    Maximum ebb through Harlow Narrows: 3.8 knots, running seaward past the cannery pier. Water temperature 44°F. The Coast Guard’s survival table gives fifty minutes. Their recovery table gives nothing at all.

    [E‑7]
  11. 23:50

    Deputy Ray Deering, on routine patrol, logs a flatbed he knows by sight: southbound on Cannery Road, lights on, moving slow. Brandt’s. Brandt’s sworn statement puts him at the Elks until midnight. The Elks were locked at 9:20.

    [E‑11, DOC‑04, DOC‑05]three men, one truck, two hours nobody owns. —H.Q.
  12. 07:30FRI

    Frances telephones the cottage. Four rings, then the operator offers to try again later. She does. Nothing.

    [DOC‑07]
  13. 09:15FRI

    The tan Valiant is found in the landing lot, doors unlocked, hood cold, windshield under a night’s snow. Nobody drove it anywhere.

    [E‑1, P‑1]
  14. 10:40FRI

    Deputy Deering enters the cottage. The kettle has boiled dry and split its seam; the ring is still on LOW — the fire marshal calls it luck. The purse hangs on its hook with eleven dollars in it. The grey cardigan is gone. The cat is waiting at the door.

    [E‑5, P‑4]
  15. —

    No confirmed sighting of Iris Ann Calloway after 9:20 P.M., January 17, 1974.

WHAT QUIST KEPT

Four notes from the investigating detective’s personal jacket, 1974–1985. Not part of the official record. Filed anyway.

MEMO TO FILE — 22 JANUARY 1974 — H. QUIST

Five days. The county wants it written up as a voluntary departure: unhappy woman buys a ticket, takes the boat, starts over in Seattle. Fine, except she never took the boat. The ticket wasn’t clipped. Holt has clipped every ticket on that run for nineteen years; he clipped tickets through the ’62 storm with the deck under water.

The ticket bothers me. You buy a ticket at 9:12 and walk north. A ticket is a story you leave behind for other people to read. Somebody wanted us to read this one.

MEMO TO FILE — 14 MARCH 1975 — H. QUIST

One-year review. Prosecutor says: no body, no crime scene, no charge. The steno pad shows two sets of figures eleven thousand dollars apart and the pages that would name the accounts are torn out. Ondine’s books were re-typed clean before the examiner arrived February 4. Re-typed by whom? The bookkeeper was gone.

Brandt sat across from me for two hours and never once asked what I thought happened to her. Innocent men ask.

MEMO TO FILE — 2 NOVEMBER 1979 — H. QUIST

Five years. The cannery shut last spring; the pier is condemned now, gulls own it. Frances still sets a place at Thursday dinner. I still take the 9:40 over some Thursdays and stand where Coyle stood and watch who walks north. Nobody walks north. There was never anything north but the pier gate and the water.

My wife says I bring the file home in my face. She’s right. I re-pinned the board again this winter. The string knows where it goes by now.

MEMO TO FILE — 30 JUNE 1985 — H. QUIST (FINAL)

I retire Friday. This file goes to the archive with the board photographed and the string re-tied the way I had it. Whoever signs the register after me: the case is one phone call, one matchbook, one unclipped ticket, one truck on a road it had no business on, and a tide that ran seaward at four knots. Put them in that order and there is only one story.

I know. Knowing isn’t proof. That is the whole difference between my job and the truth, and I retire owing the difference to Iris Calloway.

If you’re reading this, the file is yours now.
Look at the ticket again. —H.Q.
