The five heats

The temperatures are real, and the colours are what those temperatures look like on steel. This is the entire craft. Everything else is repetition.

  1. Heat 1

    Forge

    1 250 °C

    bright yellow

    Out of the fire soft as clay. The first blows do the shaping, and you have perhaps forty seconds before the bar drops out of yellow and starts arguing back.

  2. Heat 2

    Draw out

    1 050 °C

    orange

    The bar lengthens a few millimetres a blow and the section squares up. Rhythm beats force — the anvil returns half of every strike, if you let it.

  3. Heat 3

    Normalize

    830 °C

    cherry · three cycles

    Past critical, then cooled in still air, three times. Each cycle shrinks the grain — and fine grain is the difference between a blade and a bar that merely looks like one.

  4. Heat 4

    Quench

    815 °C

    cherry, then violence

    Edge-first into warmed oil. The surface flashes flame for half a second; the steel falls from 815° to under 200° in four, and comes out Rockwell 65 — glass-hard, and nearly as brittle.

  5. Heat 5

    Temper

    205 °C

    straw gold · 2 h, twice

    Two hours in the oven, twice over. Polished steel passes pale straw and is pulled at straw gold — 205° exactly, Rockwell 61. Hard enough to hold an edge, tough enough to keep it.

A different thermometer for low heat — oxide colours on polished steel. One shop, one rule: pull it at straw.
