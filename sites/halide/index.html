The process

▸ 22

Develop

Chemistry mixed fresh every morning. C-41 runs at 37.8° in the Jobo; black & white goes by hand in steel tanks — D-76, Rodinal, or a sixty-minute stand if the roll asks for it. Push or pull to ±3 stops, logged on your sleeve.

▸ 22A

Scan

Every frame passes the Noritsu at up to 4,800 px, dust knocked down by hand, no auto-crop, borders kept on request. Files land in your inbox by six the same evening — flat, honest, ready to grade.

▸ 23

Print

Fiber-based silver gelatin under the Durst L1200. Dodged and burned the way the negative asks, toned in selenium, washed a full hour, dried flat overnight between blotters. The good ones outlive us all.

Every roll leaves with one

The contact sheet

Ruth proofs each roll on a single sheet of RC paper and marks it up under the loupe. Her grease pencil does the talking: a circle means print it, a strike means let it go, and 16×20 means she already knows.

The lab

Émile Charbonneau opened Halide in March 1987 with a used Durst enlarger, a loan from his sister, and a lease nobody else wanted — three rooms behind a shuttered camera store on Fenwick Lane. Ruth Okafor came in 1991 to run black & white and never left.

Between them: roughly 1.4 million rolls, four floods, two chemical spills worth mentioning, and one wall of prints they will not sell at any price. The Jobo arrived in '98, the Noritsu in '07. The safelights are older than most of the customers.

Digital came for the neighborhood in 2003. Halide stayed. Somebody had to.
