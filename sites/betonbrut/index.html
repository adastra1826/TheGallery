Le béton n’est pas une finition. C’est la météo, arrêtée.

Concrete is not a finish. It is the weather, held still. We are fourteen architects, engineers and one concrete lab in a former ship-chandlery on the Madrague-Ville docks. Since 2011 we have poured chapels, libraries, housing and one lighthouse base — always raw, always cast on site, always stripped to show the grain of the boards that held it.

The Mistral does our weathering. The sea does our polishing. Every project leaves this office as a cyanotype and comes back as a photograph.

FICHE Nº 07 — CAP CROISETTE, MARSEILLE 8ᴱ

CHAPELLE DU RESSAC

MISE EN PLAN▽ NIVEAU +00.00 M000%

A single folded shell of white concrete, sixty seats, one slit of light. The nave is aligned on the winter-solstice sunset: at 17:04 on 21 December the sun lands exactly on the altar — a nine-tonne block lifted from the sea floor of the old Montredon quarry.

The shell was board-formed in rough Aleppo pine and records every knot. Salt spray has already begun to darken the west face. That is the elevation we drew first.

FICHE Nº 11 — CALANQUE DE SORMIOU, MARSEILLE 9ᴱ

BIBLIOTHÈQUE DE LA FALAISE

MISE EN PLAN▽ NIVEAU +00.00 M000%

A reading room cantilevered fourteen metres off the limestone, held by two prestressed ties drilled thirty-six metres back into the cliff. There is no road. Readers arrive by boat, or by the customs trail — forty minutes on foot, and worth it.

Twenty-four concrete fins ration the southern light to a steady 210 lux at the tables. On rough days the boom of the swell climbs through the slab; we tuned the fin spacing so it arrives as a low G, not a rattle.

FICHE Nº 04 — SAINT-HENRI, MARSEILLE 16ᴱ

CHÂTEAU D’EAU HABITÉ

MISE EN PLAN▽ NIVEAU +00.00 M000%

A 1954 water tower, decommissioned in 1998, recast as nine dwellings. We kept every centimetre of the original drum and cut ninety-one new openings with a diamond saw — the cores are numbered and stacked in the courtyard as benches.

Balconies rake the seaward flank on cast-in corbels. The lift runs outside, in a new oxide-tinted tower, so the original shaft never had to be touched. The old 600 m³ tank is now a cistern-garden that irrigates the whole quarter.

MÉTHODE

  1. PHASE 01

    COFFRAGE

    We build the building’s negative first, in wood. The carpenters are half our payroll, and the formwork drawings run to more sheets than the building’s.

  2. PHASE 02

    FERRAILLAGE

    Rust is structure. Every bar is bent on site, photographed, and archived before it disappears — the building keeps its skeleton a secret.

  3. PHASE 03

    COULAGE

    One lift, one day, no joint we didn’t draw. The mixer leaves Saint-Menet at 05:40; by noon the pour is vibrated, struck level, and left alone.

  4. PHASE 04

    DÉCOFFRAGE

    The only reveal that matters. We strip at dawn, when the grey is honest, and we keep the first board of every job — signed by the crew.
