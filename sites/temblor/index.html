ARCHIVE Nº 1989-0804 ■ ALVARADO FAULT RUPTURE

FIVE SECONDS
OF WARNING

At 04:15:22 UTC on 4 August 1989 — a quarter past nine in the evening, local, the shops on Calle Ancha an hour closed — a thirty-four-kilometre stretch of the Alvarado Fault ruptured at a depth of 9.2 km beneath Cerro Colorado. Station ALV-03, 42 kilometres to the southeast, kept writing through all of it. The pen tore across four neighbouring drum lines and the record survived. It is the most requested page in our archive.

The P wave, a compressional pulse travelling at 6.1 km/s, reached the vault 7.1 seconds after rupture began — a sharp knock, more heard than felt. The S wave — slower, transverse, carrying most of the energy — arrived 5.1 seconds later and did the damage. That gap is the oldest trick in seismology: multiply the S−P interval by eight and you have the distance to the source in kilometres. In earthquake country it is also, almost exactly, how long you have to get under the table.

SHAKING IS NOT
A CIRCLE

Intensity is the shadow a fault casts. The 1989 rupture propagated northwest to southeast, so its isoseismals stretch along strike — Alvarado, thirteen kilometres from the epicentre, took MMI VIII while Las Brisas, off-axis at fifty-five, barely rattled its glassware at V. Every chart you have ever seen flattens the magnitude scale with a logarithm. Below, we refuse. Four earthquakes are drawn at one common gain: M 4.0 is a hairline. MERIDIAN VBB-120 ■ VAULT INSTALLATION 2011-03-14

THE INSTRUMENT

A force-balance seismometer does not swing like a pendulum; it refuses to move. Electromagnetic feedback holds a 210-gram proof mass perfectly still against the shaking earth, and the current required to hold it is the signal. The VBB-120 resolves ground motion smaller than the width of a hydrogen atom — and it rode out 8.4 cm/s in 1989 without losing lock.

WHEN IT SHAKES

DROP

Get low before the shaking knocks you down. Hands and knees — you can still move, and you cannot fall.

COVER

Under a sturdy table, head and neck shielded. No table? Inside wall, away from glass — never a doorway.

HOLD ON

Grip a table leg and keep the cover over you — in strong shaking, furniture walks across the room.

THE PAUSE IS THE WARNING. A sharp jolt, then a beat of quiet? That was the P wave, and the S wave is on its way. Every second of that pause is eight kilometres of distance — and time you should already be spending under the table.
