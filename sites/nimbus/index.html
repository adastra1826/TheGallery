ADOPTED ON THE GRASS · 14 JUNE 1957 · AMENDED ONCE (1974), AFTER AN INCIDENT WITH AN UMBRELLA

The Manifesto

  1. i.

    We hold the sky to be the largest free exhibition on Earth — open every hour of every day, admission nothing, the entire collection replaced by weather.

  2. ii.

    We pledge to fight blue-sky thinking wherever we find it. A cloudless sky is a gallery between shows, and we will say so in meetings.

  3. iii.

    We refuse to apologise for standing in public with our heads back and our mouths slightly open. This is the correct posture for the work.

  4. iv.

    We maintain that a person lying on a hill, naming what floats by, is not wasting an afternoon. The afternoon is being spent exactly as issued.

  5. v.

    Rain is a cloud keeping its promise, and members will treat it with corresponding respect. Umbrellas are permitted. Complaints are not.

Membership stands at 12,404 — plus everyone who has ever pointed.

FIVE GENERA · ASCENDING ORDER OF ALTITUDE

The Plates

Ascending altitude is also, as it happens, the order of the day: low cloud belongs to morning, and high ice keeps the last light. The atlas simply follows the sun.

I

PLATE I · THE LOW DECK · 0–600 M · BEST VIEWED 06:12

Stratus nebulosus

From the Latin sternere, to spread or strew: a cloud laid flat, past tense. The species name nebulosus — “full of mist” — is the politest thing anyone has said about it.

GENUSSTRATUS
SPECIESNEBULOSUS
BAND0–0.6 KM AGL
BUILT OFWATER, 0.3 G/M³
COVER8/8 OKTAS
YIELDSDRIZZLE, AT MOST

FOLKLORE

Hill shepherds promise that “a grey lid at dawn lifts by dinner.” The Society has tested the claim on 214 mornings since 1961. It holds roughly half the time — which, for weather folklore, is an outstanding record.

SCIENCE

Stratus forms when a shallow, stable layer of moist air cools to its dew point from below. It is fog that has managed to clear its feet from the ground, and it is no more ambitious than that: no convection, no edges, no drama.

Spotter’s note — this plate is complete. Nothing has been omitted. Stratus is the sky declining to perform: one cloud, wall to wall, all morning. The Society admires the honesty of it.

II

PLATE II · FAIR-WEATHER HEAPS · 600–2 000 M · BEST VIEWED 09:41

Cumulus humilis

From cumulus, a heap, and humilis, humble — low to the ground, sharing a root with “humility” and “humus.” A modest pile of morning.

GENUSCUMULUS
SPECIESHUMILIS
BAND0.6–2.0 KM AGL
BUILT OFWATER DROPLETS
LIFESPAN≈ 15 MIN / PUFF
YIELDSSHADE, BRIEFLY

FOLKLORE

“Cumulus at nine, the hay will be fine.” The fair-weather heap is the countryman’s barometer set permanently to REASSURING: so long as each cloud stays wider than it is tall, the picnic proceeds.

SCIENCE

Each puff is the flag on top of an invisible thermal — a column of warm air rising off a sunlit field. The cloud is not the engine; it is the exhaust. Watch one for fifteen minutes: it frays, evaporates, and an entirely new cloud is issued in its place. What persists is the field below.

Spotter’s note — the puffs in this plate are on the fifteen-minute plan as well. No cloud you are looking at now will outlast the chapter.

III

PLATE III · THE TOWER · 500–12 800 M · BEST VIEWED 15:26

Cumulonimbus capillatus incus

Cumulus again — the heap — now with nimbus, rain, bolted on. Incus is the anvil, and the blacksmith connotation is fully earned.

GENUSCUMULONIMBUS
SUPPL.CAPILLATUS INCUS
BAND0.5–12.8 KM
UPDRAUGHT25 M/S, CLIMBING
CHARGEGRAUPEL ON ICE
YIELDSEVERYTHING AT ONCE
ADVISORY: OBSERVE FROM A HILL YOU ARE PREPARED TO LEAVE.

FOLKLORE

Count the seconds between flash and thunder, divide by three: kilometres to the storm. Old, simple, and correct — sound covers a kilometre every three seconds, and folklore occasionally files an accurate report.

SCIENCE

The only cloud that occupies the whole ruler. A tower of rising air punches upward until it strikes the tropopause — the ceiling of weather — and, unable to go higher, spreads sideways into the anvil: a storm drawing its own roof. Inside, ice and graupel collide until the charge has nowhere left to go. Then it goes.

Spotter’s note — this specimen is live and keeps its own schedule. The Society does not hurry it, and neither will you.

IV

PLATE IV · THE STANDING WAVE · 2 000–7 000 M · BEST VIEWED 19:03

Altocumulus lenticularis

From lenticula, a little lentil — lens-shaped. The French call it pile d’assiettes, a stack of plates. Everyone else calls the Society to report a saucer.

GENUSALTOCUMULUS
SPECIESLENTICULARIS
BAND2.0–7.0 KM AGL
WIND THROUGH80 KM/H
MOTIONNONE. SEE SCIENCE
SAUCER FILE61 REPORTS SINCE 1957

FOLKLORE

The Society keeps a file, four fingers thick, of flying-saucer sightings later resolved to lenticularis. We answer every letter the same way: what you saw was better than a spacecraft, and it was made of air.

SCIENCE

Downwind of a ridge, the airstream bounces the way water does past a stone. Where each wave crests, moisture condenses; where it dips, the cloud dissolves. The wind pours through at eighty kilometres an hour while the shape holds perfectly still — it is not an object at all, but a place where the sky keeps happening.

Spotter’s note — a stationary cloud in a moving sky. If it is hovering, it is ours. If it lands, telephone somebody else.

V

PLATE V · MARES’ TAILS · 6 000–13 000 M · BEST VIEWED 21:48

Cirrus uncinus

Cirrus is a curl of hair; uncinus, hooked. Sailors called the comma-shaped streaks mares’ tails, and read them the way we read headlines.

GENUSCIRRUS
SPECIESUNCINUS
BAND6–13 KM AGL
BUILT OFICE, FALLING
TEMPERATURE−40 TO −60 °C
WARM FRONT24–36 H BEHIND

FOLKLORE

“Mackerel sky and mares’ tails make tall ships carry low sails.” Cirrus streaming ahead of a warm front really does promise rain within a day or two. The proverb verifies about two times in three, and the Society has the ledgers.

SCIENCE

Each streak is a fall of ice: crystals dropping from a high tuft into slower wind below, combed sideways into hooks by the shear. Six kilometres of altitude buys half an hour of extra sunlight — which is why cirrus burns pink long after the ground has gone grey. It is the last cloud to say goodnight.

Spotter’s note — when this plate goes dark, the day is over. The Society thanks you for scrolling it kindly.

THREE INSTRUMENTS, ALL FREE

Field Method

THE OKTA

Sky cover is counted in eighths. Lie back, divide the dome, count the eighths with cloud in them. Zero is a clear sky — see Article ii for the Society’s position — and eight is a stratus morning, countable in one glance.

0 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8

THE FIST

A fist held at arm’s length spans ten degrees of sky, on anyone’s arm — the anatomy scales with the owner. Horizon to zenith is nine fists, and the sun sinks one fist every forty minutes. Plan the walk home accordingly.

THE THUNDER COUNT

Flash, then count the seconds to the thunder and divide by three: kilometres to the storm. If the answer falls twice in a row, the storm is coming to you. See Plate III, advisory.
