A tea room in
the mountains.

Seifū-an is four and a half mats at the top of a cedar ridge — two seatings most days, four guests at most, tea made the slow way.

It is summer at the ridge. The path is deep green, the stream is loud, and the room stays cool until evening.

The room

Four and a half mats, one window.

The room was raised in 1957 by the carpenter Ōta Kanjirō and has been rebuilt twice since, both times with the same cedar. The ceiling is woven bark. The tokonoma holds one scroll and one flower, changed for every seating. A low window faces the ravine; on clear mornings the light reaches the third mat, and that is the whole show.

The fire is laid an hour before you arrive. Water comes from the spring behind the room and boils in an iron kettle from Morioka. You will hear it — the sound has a name, matsukaze, wind in the pines — and for a while it is the only sound.

At the door

Shoes stay on the stone step; the room is entered in socks or tabi — we keep clean spares in three sizes. Watches, phones, and rings rest in a paulownia box in the waiting arbor and are returned when you leave. Nothing else is asked of you. Kneeling is not required; sit as you can sit for an hour.

The seatings

Tea twice a day, four guests at most.

Asa-cha — morning tea

朝茶 ¥4,500 a guest

Daily · 06:30 · 50 minutes

Thin tea and one sweet, taken while the mountain is still deciding on the weather. The simplest seating and, in the opinion of the house, the best one.

Shōgo no chaji — the full ceremony

正午の茶事 ¥28,000 a guest

Friday – Sunday · 11:00 · about four hours

The complete form: charcoal laid before you, a small kaiseki meal, thick tea passed in one bowl, then thin tea to close. One party only. We explain each step as it comes; nothing is assumed.

Yoi no chakai — evening tea

宵の茶会 ¥6,800 a guest

Thursday – Monday · 17:00 & 18:45 · 70 minutes

Thin tea by lantern light with the day's sweet. In summer the window stays open for the evening birds; in winter, the kettle does the talking.

First visit? Say so when you book — the host serves a little slower and names each utensil as it is used.

The sweets

The confection changes with the mountain.

Wagashi are made each morning in the lower kitchen and carried up with the day's charcoal. One is served before the tea and is part of your seating; the others can be asked for after, with a second bowl.

  • 水羊羹 Mizu-yōkan ¥700

    Chilled azuki jelly set with spring water, cut cold in the well house.

  • 葛饅頭 Kuzu-manjū ¥750

    Bean paste inside a clear kudzu skin — held to the window, it shows the ravine.

  • 蕨餅 Warabi-mochi ¥700

    Bracken-starch mochi under roasted soy flour and a thread of black sugar.

The path

There is no road.

Leave the car — or the 14:10 bus from Kiso-Fukushima — at the Ōdaira foot station, where the keeper takes your luggage and anything you would rather not carry. The path climbs 310 metres in about forty minutes: packed earth through the cedars, then eighty-two stone steps for the last stretch. There are three benches on the way and one spring you can drink from.

From dusk the steps are lit with paper lanterns. In snow we come down and walk up with you — say so when you book, and the morning seating moves from 06:30 to 08:00. Umbrellas, sticks, and dry socks wait in the arbor at the top.

The climb is honest but not hard. If stairs are a difficulty, write to us first; there is a longer, gentler way used by the kettle-carrier.

Reservations

Four seats a seating, held quietly.

Reservations open on the first morning of each month for the month following. We hold seats rather than take deposits; if your plans change, three days' notice is enough.

No payment is taken now; you pay at the room.
