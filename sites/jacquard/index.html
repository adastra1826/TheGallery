The draft room

Draft your own cloth.

A draft is a weaving program, three grids long. The threading assigns each warp end to a shaft. The tie-up wires shafts to treadles. The treadling plays the treadles in order. Toggle any cell — the drawdown weaves your pattern at once, and the card chain in the 1801 room is repunched to match.

Reading order: the threading sends warp end 14 to shaft 3; the tie-up wires treadle 2 to shafts 3 and 4; when the treadling fires treadle 2, every end on those shafts rises and the shuttle passes beneath. That is the whole computer.

Sidebar · Lyon, 1801

The loom that taught machines to read.

In 1801 Joseph-Marie Jacquard showed a loom that read its pattern from a chain of punched cards — one card per pick, one hole per hook. A hole lifted a thread; blank card left it down. Change the cards and the same machine wove different cloth. For the first time anywhere, the pattern had become data.

To prove the point, the Lyon workshops wove a portrait of Jacquard himself in black and white silk. It took 24,000 cards, and the result looked so exactly like an engraving that Charles Babbage hung a copy in his drawing room to unsettle his guests — then borrowed the card chain outright for the Analytical Engine.

“The Analytical Engine weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves.” Ada Lovelace, 1843

These cards are compiled, live, from your draft in the room above. If you toggled a single cell up there, you have already programmed a machine today.

Commissions

From sitting to selvedge in five steps.

  1. 01

    The sitting

    Bring a use, not a design: a window that needs softening, a coat that wants a cloth, a table that seats ten. We talk fibre, weight and wear for three quarters of an hour, at the mill or by letter.

    45 min · free
  2. 02

    Draft & sample

    Nell drafts three structures against your use and weaves a 30 cm strip of each on the trial loom. You keep the strips. Cloth should be argued with in the hand, never on a screen.

    2 weeks · €120, credited
  3. 03

    Warping

    We wind your warp end by end at the warping wall — 480 ends of twelve metres is a day and a half of counting aloud — then dress the loom: threading, sleying, tying on.

    3 days at the wall
  4. 04

    Weaving

    Marta weaves a disciplined metre a day, twills slower, satins slower still. You are welcome on Thursdays to stand where the noise is and watch your yardage grow.

    ≈ 1 m per day
  5. 05

    Finishing

    No cloth is finished off the loom. It is washed, dried under tension and pressed hard — it goes into the bath as string and comes out as fabric.

    1 week
