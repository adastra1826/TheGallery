0° → 90° · THE GENERAL CIRCULATION

The Engine

The sun does not simply heat the Earth; it overheats the middle of it. Every day the tropics bank more light than they can spend, and the poles run cold at a loss. Wind is the transfer of that unbalanced account — warm air rising off the equator, sliding poleward ten kilometres up, sinking back near 30° as the dry, desert-making weight of the subtropics.

Nothing moves in a straight line on a spinning planet. In 1835 Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis wrote down the deflection that bears his name, and the sky obeys it everywhere: rightward in the north, leftward in the south. The result is not chaos but architecture — three great cells of turning air in each hemisphere, stacked neatly from equator to pole.

Wind is the sky settling a debt the sun rewrites every morning.

30°N → 5°N · THE NORTHEAST TRADES

The Trades

Between the subtropics and the equator the sky exhales in one direction only. The trade winds lean out of the northeast above the line and the southeast below it, day after day, at a walking-to-cycling pace of fourteen to twenty-two kilometres per hour. They are the steadiest wind on Earth — not the strongest; the most dependable.

Sailors learned their timetable long before anyone could explain it. Columbus dropped south to the Canaries in 1492 to catch them west. From 1565 the Manila galleons rode the belt across the Pacific for two hundred and fifty years — an empire rigged to a breeze. The name is older than commerce: trade once meant path, or track. This wind kept one.

For four centuries, empires kept their appointments by a wind you could set a watch to.

5°N → 5°S · THE INTERTROPICAL CONVERGENCE

The Doldrums

Where the two trade belts meet, they cancel. Air arrives from both hemispheres, collides, and — with nowhere else to go — goes up. The Intertropical Convergence Zone is a seam of rising air stitched loosely along five degrees north, wandering with the seasons, boiling with thunderheads and almost perfectly still at the surface.

Square-rigged ships could drift here for weeks, sails slack, fresh water souring in the casks. Coleridge gave the stillness its literature — as idle as a painted ship upon a painted ocean — but the calm is a trick of perspective. The doldrums hold some of the most violently vertical weather on Earth. The wind has not died here.

The wind does not stop in the doldrums. It turns, and climbs.

40°E → 100°E · CROSS-EQUATORIAL FLOW

The Monsoon

Twice a year, over the Indian Ocean, the whole system changes its mind. In summer the Asian landmass heats faster than the sea, the pressure gradient flips, and the southeast trades of the other hemisphere are dragged across the equator, bent rightward, and delivered to India as a river of ocean air out of the southwest.

The word comes from the Arabic mausim — season — because for a thousand years dhow captains ran the Africa–India trade on its schedule: out in June, home in December. The southwest monsoon still delivers roughly eighty percent of India's annual rainfall between June and September. It is the only wind on this page that a billion people wait for by name.

Twice a year, half the sky reverses — and a subcontinent looks up.

35°N → 60°N · 9–12 KM ALOFT

The Jet Stream

Nine to twelve kilometres up, where cold polar air presses against warm subtropical air, the boundary itself begins to run. The polar jet is a ribbon of wind a few hundred kilometres wide and only a few deep, meandering eastward at one hundred to four hundred kilometres per hour — faster than any gale that ever touched the ground.

Wasaburo Oishi charted it first, tracking weather balloons from the Tateno observatory through the 1920s; he published in Esperanto, and the world ignored him. American B-29 crews rediscovered it the hard way in 1944, opening bomb runs into a headwind outrunning their airspeed indicators. Today every eastbound flight you take drafts quietly in its wake.

A river with no banks, running faster than the land below will ever allow.
