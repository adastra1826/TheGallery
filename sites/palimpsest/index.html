§ essay

What the Knife Leaves.

E. Maravall, Keeper of Manuscripts — read at the winter colloquium of the Comité international de paléographie, Geneva, 30 January 2026.

A monastery kitchen ledger will tell you what a book cost before it was a book: sixty-eight sheep for one lectern Bible, dressed and limed and scraped thin through a Burgundian winter.1 Parchment was land, weather and labour pressed flat enough to write on. When the words on a skin stopped earning their keep — the liturgy reformed, the language died, the mathematics went out of fashion — the skin was still worth a flock. So the knife came out, and the pumice after it, and the page went back to being a field.

The scribe who erased the treatise now on our bench did nothing shameful by his own lights. He was recycling. But iron-gall ink is not a coating; it is a provoked chemistry — oak-gall tannin set against green vitriol — and it does not rest on parchment so much as bite into it, forty to eighty microns down into the collagen weave.2 A knife can take the body of a letter. It cannot reach the grave.

Ultraviolet is how we read the grave. At 365 nanometres, parchment fluoresces: the skin answers invisible light with a pale blue-white of its own making. Where old ink once etched the collagen, the answer fails — fluorescence is quenched, and the dead letters print themselves as shadows on the glow.3 Note what this means. We never photograph the erased ink; there is no ink left to photograph. We photograph the skin’s memory of being written on. The text survives the way a scar survives.

ὅπερ ἔδει δεῖξαι.
“Which was to be proved.” The last legible words of the undertext — recovered 8 November 2024, 21:41, band 2 of 12.

It is tempting to resent the prayer book for standing on the mathematics. That has it backwards. The Hours are why this vellum stayed bound, dry, shelved and wanted for eight centuries; erasure was the rent the treatise paid for shelter. Nearly every great recovered undertext survives by the same unhappy bargain — the Method of Archimedes lived inside a prayer book for seven hundred years precisely because somebody kept valuing the prayers.4 The eraser and the author are co-donors to the archive. Only one of them meant to be.

So the laboratory does not take sides. We will not lift the Hours to reach the Greek, and we will not pretend the Greek is not there. The folio leaves the bench exactly as it arrived — one skin, two voices, eight centuries apart — except that now both can be heard at once, each in its own light. What the knife left, the lamp returns.
