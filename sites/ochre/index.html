12 m · the twilight zone

Past the reach of the sun

Beyond this point no daylight has ever fallen. Every image in the Vayrac cave was made by someone holding fire — a juniper-wick lamp or a resin-soaked pine torch, a pool of light two metres wide, carried two hundred and sixty metres into the mountain. This archive reproduces that condition faithfully. We could floodlight the survey scans. We choose not to.

Seven painted works survive along the passage. Hold your light on a wall until it answers, and what you find is yours: the archive keeps your traverse, panel by panel, until you extinguish it at the door. Most visitors leave something in the dark.

Galerie des mains — 87 m move your light along the wall

VAY-P01 Galerie des mains · 87 m

Twenty-seven hands, none of them drawn

No brush touched the wall here. Each hand was pressed flat against the limestone while pigment — red hematite or yellow goethite, ground fine and slaked with cave water — was sprayed over it from the mouth in a fast, exhaled cloud. The hand lifted away; its absence stayed. Twenty-seven negative stencils crowd the panel: nineteen left hands, eight right, exactly what you would expect of right-handed people holding the spray pipe in the dominant hand.

Four hands fold the last two fingers below the knuckle. The same folded-finger forms repeat at Gargas and Cosquer, five hundred kilometres apart — a signal code, most researchers now argue, not mutilation. And at a height of 2.1 metres, far above any adult’s flat-footed reach, one stencil is eleven centimetres long. A child of five, perhaps six. Someone stood in the dark and lifted them up.

16 October 1954

The boy, the dog, the storm

The storm crossed the Causse on the night of the 13th, and by morning a sinkhole had opened in the Delmas sheep pasture above Vayrac — a slot of black air where yesterday there had been grass. Rémy Cassagne, thirteen, was sent up to count the ewes. He brought the farm dog, Bécassine, which is the only reason any of this happened: she went down the slot after the smell of cold stone, and she would not come back.

He walked home for the bicycle lamp and a pocketful of matches, and told no one. The lamp was weak and yellow. He followed the barking down a scree slope, across a chamber whose ceiling his light never found, and along a passage that swallowed every sound except hers. At what the survey now calls 87 metres, he raised the lamp to look for a way up the wall. The wall was covered in hands.

He came back the next morning with his schoolmaster, M. Delfau, and a paraffin lantern. By 3 November, Professor Hélène Marsac of the Institut de Paléontologie Humaine had descended the doline, matched the calcite skin over the pigment against every forgery test she had, and wired Paris four words: authentic — seal the field. Her monograph Vayrac I (1958) is still the reference. Rémy carried her instruments on every campaign until the cave closed.

“People always ask whether I was afraid. Not of the dark — of the hands. The lamp found them all at once, and they were exactly the size of mine.”

— Rémy Cassagne, interviewed for the fortieth anniversary, 1994

Rotonde de l’aurochs — 141 m

VAY-P02 Rotonde de l’aurochs · 141 m

Three and a half metres of charging bull

The aurochs takes the whole east wall of the rotunda. The outline went up in one campaign — pine-charcoal strokes as wide as four centimetres, laid down without a single hesitation the survey can detect — then the shoulder and flank were washed with hematite pressed in by pad. The horns are drawn in twisted perspective, both curving forward while the head runs in profile: a convention shared with Lascaux, not an error. Under raking light, ten faint engraved lines shadow the back and chest. The painter sketched first. Then committed.

Pigment analysis · campaigns 1996 / 2011

The laboratory reads the wall

Nothing enters the cave now except instruments. Portable X-ray fluorescence and Raman spectroscopy go down the doline twice a year, carried by two conservators in clean suits who are budgeted six hours of breath each. This is what the wall has told them.

Either the pigment travelled, or the painters did. Both readings mean the same thing: the dark places of the valley were talking to each other.

Panneau des chevaux — 203 m

VAY-P03 Panneau des chevaux · 203 m

Spotted horses, and the argument they ended

Two horses stand rump to rump, small-headed and heavy-bellied, their coats stippled with manganese-black dapples that spill past the outlines onto bare rock. For a century the dots were read as anything but dots — trance patterns, star maps, abstraction. Then, in 2011, palaeogeneticists recovered the leopard-spotting gene from Pleistocene horse bone. Spotted horses were real, and the painters had watched them. The panel is not a vision. It is a portrait.

Beside the horses: a claviform sign and a pair of red dots that repeat, identically, in four other caves of the valley. A vocabulary we can map and cannot read.

Le Puits — 260 m the deepest light in the cave

VAY-P04 Le Puits · 260 m

The one human figure

At the end of the painted passage the floor simply stops: a shaft, six metres down, walls of wet flowstone. At its base is the only human figure in the cave — a man with a bird’s head, arms thrown wide, tipping backwards before a wounded bison whose entrails loop below it like rope. A bird on a staff stands apart, watching. Nothing else at Vayrac is narrative; this is.

On the ledge beside the scene the 1955 campaign found a carved limestone lamp, catalogue VAY-L01, its bowl still greasy, its wick of juniper. Someone climbed down with fire to paint the hardest picture in the cave, and left the light behind when they went up. Above the drop, four parallel claw-marks score the flowstone — cave bear, and older than everything painted here.

You are 260 metres from the sun. Whatever looks back at you here has been waiting seventeen thousand years.

The ascent · 1957 – 1979

Breath is the enemy

Vayrac opened to the public in June 1957 — a concrete stair down the doline, a steel handrail through the galleries, floodlights on the aurochs. One hundred and eighteen thousand people came in 1972 alone, and every one of them exhaled: warmth, carbon dioxide, moisture, and the spores that ride on daylight. By 1974, green algae haloed every lamp — conservators call it lampenflora, as if naming it could tame it. By 1977 a calcite veil, measurable in microns and growing, was clouding the Panel of the Hands. The rock ran 2.6 °C above its ten-thousand-year baseline.

Lascaux had already shown everyone how the story ends. On 12 April 1979 the Direction du Patrimoine ordered Vayrac sealed behind a climate door, and the stair was broken out behind it. A facsimile was proposed in 1991; the commune voted it down, sixty-one to fourteen. Vayrac chose absence.

The paintings survived one hundred and seventy centuries. They very nearly did not survive twenty-five years of being loved. The door is not a punishment. The door is the treatment.

Rémy Cassagne stood at the entrance the morning it closed — thirty-eight years old, the only person alive who had seen the hands by bicycle lamp. “I found it,” he told the prefect, “so I get to say this: lock it well.”
