The Collection

Star specimens

Every plant in the collection carries an accession number, a provenance, and a story about the journey here. These three draw the crowds — and one of them is about to do something it does once a decade.

Acc. № 1889.117 · Palm House, centre bed

Amorphophallus titanum

the titan arum — “the Wrenfold Giant”

Collected as a corm the size of a footstool, Lampung, Sumatra, 1889; shipped home in a tea chest stencilled DO NOT WATER.

— days until predicted bloom
vespertine · one night only · members telephoned in alphabetical order

Our seventh bloom in one hundred and thirty-seven years. The spathe opens after dark and smells, famously, of things best left undescribed; the society keeps late hours for the occasion. The queue starts forming when the spadix passes 36 degrees — it heats itself, the show-off.

  • HEIGHT 2.67 M
  • GAINING 11 CM / DAY
  • SPADIX 36.1 °C

Fig. 2 — the pond, seen from the gallery

Acc. № 1877.002 · Waterlily House, the round pond

Victoria amazonica

the giant waterlily

Seed sent from the Rupununi River, Guyana, 1877, packed in damp moss inside a sea captain’s snuff tin.

The pads reach 2.8 metres across and will carry forty kilograms if you spread the load — a claim tested annually at the Members’ Fête with a folding chair, a borrowed child, and no incidents since 1962. The flower opens white for one night, imprisons its beetle pollinators until morning, blushes crimson, and lets them go without apology.

  • PAD Ø 2.8 M
  • BEARS 40 KG
  • FLOWER: TWO NIGHTS, TWO COLOURS

Fig. 3 — location within the case withheld

Acc. № 1902.044 · Orchid Case, far end

Dendrophylax lindenii

the ghost orchid

Fakahatchee Strand, Florida, 1902; three crossings failed before one arrived alive, lashed to a slab of pond-apple bark.

No leaves. No visible means of support. The entire plant is a handful of grey roots that photosynthesise on their own, and once a year — if the fog is right — it produces a white flower that appears to hover in mid-air. We keep it behind glass within glass, and we do not tell visitors exactly where.

  • LEAVES: NONE
  • ROOTS: PHOTOSYNTHETIC
  • LOCATION: UNDISCLOSED

The Propagator

Notes from the potting bench

Vol. CLIII · № 7 · July 2026 · circulated to members by post, second-class, as tradition demands

4 July 2026

On the arum, again

The spadix has passed two and a half metres and the betting book in the potting shed now runs to four pages. Mr Osei has the 26th; I have the 24th, and shall say no more. When it opens we will telephone the membership in alphabetical order, as ever — so if your name is Zysman, I apologise in advance.

— Edith Marchbank, Head Keeper

21 June 2026

On fern spore, and patience

The Osmunda spore sown in March has germinated: a green film on the pan no wider than a thumbprint. In two years it will be a fern; in five, a specimen. Members asking after “quick results” are directed to the nasturtiums.

— E.M.

9 June 2026

A caution regarding tendrils

The Passiflora in the Temperate Corridor has taken hold of the barometer again. Nothing that grips with that much confidence should be trusted near instruments. Pruning is Fridays; gloves are in the third drawer; courage is your own affair.

— E.M.
