der stil — the style

In 1918 Ernst Keller began teaching graphic design at the Kunstgewerbeschule Zürich with a rule that sounds modest and proved radical: the solution to a design problem must be found inside its content. Between that classroom and the export boom of the 1960s, Swiss designers assembled the first genuinely international visual language — objective, systematic, and anonymous by conviction. Its makers called it konstruktive Grafik. The world came to call it Swiss.

The system rested on four commitments. A mathematical grid ordered the sheet before any content arrived. Sans-serif letters — Akzidenz-Grotesk at first, then the new grotesques of 1957 — spoke in what Zürich believed was no accent at all. Photography displaced illustration, because a photograph testifies while a drawing persuades. And text sat flush left, ragged right, its spacing as even as coursed masonry.

Two temperaments shared the doctrine. Zürich — Josef Müller-Brockmann, Richard Paul Lohse — wanted construction: the poster as a visible calculation. Basel — Armin Hofmann, Emil Ruder — trained the eye and hand until reduction became reflex. Between the two schools, Geigy's pharmaceutical graphics, airline timetables, and the trilingual quarterly Neue Grafik carried the argument across every border it was designed to ignore.

By 1974 the style had won so completely that it disappeared into infrastructure — signage, forms, timetables, corporate identity — while its own students in Basel were already loosening the grid it was built on. Halle 10 shows eight sheets from the two decades when order was an ethical position, not a software default.

chronologie — 1953 bis 1974

  1. 1953

    The Hochschule für Gestaltung opens in Ulm. Max Bill, its founding rector, carries Zürich's concrete-art rigor into a full curriculum — the style acquires a school beyond Switzerland.

  2. 1957

    Annus mirabilis of the grotesque: Neue Haas Grotesk (Miedinger and Hoffmann, Basel) and Univers (Frutiger) are released within months of each other.

  3. 1958

    Lohse, Müller-Brockmann, Neuburg and Vivarelli launch the quarterly Neue Grafik in Zürich — set in three languages on one unbending grid.

  4. 1960

    The Stempel foundry renames Neue Haas Grotesk for export. The new name: Helvetica. Switzerland becomes a typeface.

  5. 1961

    Müller-Brockmann publishes Gestaltungsprobleme des Grafikers, the movement's first systematic textbook.

  6. 1965

    Hofmann's Graphic Design Manual appears; Unimark International is founded and begins moving the doctrine through corporate America.

  7. 1966

    Bob Noorda and Massimo Vignelli begin rationalizing New York's subway signage. The Swiss grid goes underground.

  8. 1967

    Emil Ruder's Typographie is published in Basel — twenty years of teaching condensed into one bilingual manual.

  9. 1968

    Wolfgang Weingart starts teaching at the Basel school. The style's own seminar room begins, very carefully, to bend the rules.

  10. 1972

    Weingart tours American design schools, asking aloud whether Swiss typography can be made lively again. The audience takes notes — and liberties.

  11. 1974

    Our survey closes. The vocabulary — grid, grotesque, objective image — passes from movement into infrastructure, where it remains in every interface you used today.
