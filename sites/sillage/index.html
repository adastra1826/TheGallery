La maison

Maison Sillage occupies the old Herboristerie Verdier at 17 rue de l'Échiquier — the oak drawers still smell of gentian. Two copper alembics from Grasse, re-tinned by hand, dated 1898 and 1911. We compose at extrait strength only, twenty-eight to thirty-one percent perfume, because dilution is a decision we prefer to leave to the rain.

Aube

The hour the streetlights concede.

Bergamot pressed in Reggio di Calabria in the last dark week of November, when the peel is coldest and gives its oil without bitterness. We fold it into orange blossom from Nabeul and a Siamese benzoin that has spent nineteen weeks in tank. Worn close, it is a window opened at 6 h 40 in winter — cold citrus over yesterday's candle.

L'Heure Grise

Dusk indoors, before anyone thinks to light a lamp.

Six-year orris — three years in Chianti soil, three drying in the attic above it — milled to a butter at 34 percent irone. Violet leaf sharpens it; a white-suede accord and one clean musk put skin beneath the powder. It does not project. It waits at the collarbone and answers only those who lean in.

Minuit Vert

A walled garden at midnight, every door open.

Vetiver from Les Cayes, washed in river water and distilled for twenty-four hours over a wood fire — the smoke stays in the oil, and we keep it there. Green fig leaf and cracked black pepper above; below, only root. Fourteen weeks of maceration, then a winter at four degrees. Wear it and the room stays yours for an hour after you leave.

La méthode

Slow in every direction

Matières

Eleven farms, four countries, one buyer — Camille Aubriet, who signs at harvest, not before. We pay for the year, not the yield; a frost in Calabria is our problem too. What reaches Paris is weighed, logged, and given a line in the register below.

Macération

Compounds rest in steel for fourteen to nineteen weeks — stirred on Mondays, smelled on Thursdays. Then a false winter: three weeks at four degrees to drop the waxes, one slow filtration, and not another touch. We bottle in March and in September.

Flacon

Mouth-blown glass from Waldsassen; a ground stopper lapped to its own neck — the pair are numbered together and separate badly. Sealed with a thumbprint of black beeswax. If yours ever sticks, write to us. There is a trick with a warm cloth.
