Kiln is the mountain studio of Maren Holt — stoneware dug from the Doe River bank, thrown on a slow wheel, and fired for three days in a kiln that burns trees instead of hours.

ICentering

Nine pounds of Doe River clay, wedged thirty turns and slapped down dead center. Press until the wobble gives up and the lump runs true under your palms.

IIOpening

Thumbs find the floor of the pot, half an inch above the wheel head. It is a vessel now — everything after is a negotiation about walls.

IIIPulling

Knuckle outside, two fingers in, and the wall rises between them. Three slow pulls — the clay remembers every hurried one.

IVShaping

Pressure from the inside hand and the belly swells. Ease off, and the shoulder turns in. Collar the neck. Let the curve decide when it is done.

VFinishing

A rib firms the wall, a chamois softens the lip, a wire sings under the foot. Twelve minutes of attention — then months to earn the fire.

The philosophy

The kiln has opinions

An electric kiln repeats itself. Doe — our 310-cubic-foot anagama, named for the river below the studio — does not. For seventy-two hours, flame runs the length of her chamber and six cords of wind-fallen oak give up their minerals as ash. Where the ash lands, it melts into glass. Where the embers touch, the clay blushes iron. We glaze some pots and leave most bare, because the kiln finishes them better than we could.

This is slow work in the old sense: the wheel is slow, the drying is slower, and the firing takes a crew of nine working four-hour stoking shifts around the clock. Out of three hundred pots, perhaps forty come out the way we imagined — and a dozen come out better. Wabi-sabi is not a style here. It is an agreement with the fire about who finishes the work.

The glazes

Four ways the fire answers

Test tiles from the last twelve firings. Recipes in parts by weight. Every one of them moves under the flame — that is the point.

  • Ash

    T-118 · cone 10 · reduction

    oak ash 45 · custer feldspar 35 · ball clay 20

    Runs glassy where the flame lands; dry as bone in the shadow of other pots.

  • Doe River celadon

    T-104 · cone 10 · reduction

    river slip 40 · silica 30 · whiting 20 · red iron 2

    Blue-green where it pools, honest grey where it thins. It crazes over porcelain, and we let it.

  • Tenmoku

    T-131 · cone 10 · reduction

    alberta slip 85 · whiting 10 · red iron 5

    Black at rest, rust wherever it breaks over an edge. Best on bowls that get used every day.

  • Shino

    T-097 · cone 10 · reduction

    nepheline syenite 70 · soda ash 15 · epk 15

    Salmon to snow, and it traps carbon if you fire it hungry. The kiln decides which.

The firing

Seventy-two hours, stoked by hand

Firing 45 begins 9 October 2026 at six in the morning. Nine crew, four-hour shifts, roughly five hundred stokes. The chart is the plan — Doe will have notes.

  1. h 00Light the front firebox at dawn. Coffee. Candle overnight — steam off three hundred pots, gently.
  2. h 14The climb begins. Stoke every twelve minutes, splits of poplar, watching the color move from black to first red.
  3. h 24Body reduction: choke the damper and let the flame starve and search. This is where the iron in the clay wakes up.
  4. h 36Side-stoking starts. Ash builds on every shoulder in the front stack. Cone 04 leans, then bows.
  5. h 48Cone 8 down at the front. Four-minute stokes now — the chamber breathes with the door and the whole crew moves in rhythm.
  6. h 60Cone 10 soft everywhere. Hold. The glass needs time more than it needs heat.
  7. h 68Last stoke. Mud the door, brick the ports, seal every gap — then four days of patience while she cools.
