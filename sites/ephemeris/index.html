    The Press

    Publishers of the heavens, by appointment to no one.

    In the winter of 1853, Aurelia Voss — lately dismissed from the Harrowgate Observatory for the impertinence of publishing her own reduction tables — set six columns of figures in borrowed type and sold four hundred copies from a stationer’s window. She called the pamphlet Ephemeris, and promised her readers one thing only: that the heavens would keep the appointments printed herein.

    They have kept them for one hundred and seventy-three years since. Every rising, every occultation, every grudging minute of winter daylight in this volume is computed twice, by two computers working apart, and admitted to the page only when their figures agree. The Press still keeps Voss’s rule pinned above the composing stone: print nothing the sky will not honour.

    The Editions, 1853–2026

    One hundred and seventy-four volumes, unbroken. War thinned the paper; nothing has stopped the figures.

    Rest on a starred spine for its story. Eleven copies of the first edition survive; the Press owns three and knows where the others sleep.
