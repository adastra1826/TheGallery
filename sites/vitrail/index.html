THE ATELIER

Four glaziers, one kiln,
six centuries of method

Atelier Vitrail has worked from the same courtyard at 14 rue des Tanneurs, Troyes, since 1921. We conserve medieval and nineteenth-century windows for parishes, monuments services and private chapels, and we make new windows the old way — mouth-blown glass, painted with grisaille and silver stain, fired, and leaded by hand.

The workshop holds four benches. Marguerite Aubriot leads conservation; her condition reports are written before any pane leaves its stone. Étienne Rameau cuts and leads. Paul Vasseur paints. Odile Brissac, apprenticed here in 2019, manages the glass rack and the kiln — a 1953 Guérin, still on its original brick.

We are members of Corpus Vitrearum France and follow its conservation charter: nothing removed that can be kept, nothing added that cannot be undone, and every intervention logged, photographed and signed into the dossier that travels with the window for the rest of its life.

RESTAURATION · DOSSIER 2024-117

The Saint-Aubin lancet

Lancet n II, chapelle Saint-Aubin, Verzenay (Marne). Glass of c. 1487, restored 1861 and releaded 1907. Removed to the atelier 12 March 2024; reinstalled 9 October 2024. Drag the lead line across the lancet to move between the condition we found and the window we returned.

TREATMENT

Cleaned pane by pane with deionised water and cotton swab. Forty-one fractures edge-bonded with silicone; nine 1907 mending leads removed and replaced with copper-foil repairs invisible from three metres. The two losses were glazed in new mouth-blown glass, painted to read with the fifteenth-century hand and signed with our date on the inner face. Borders releaded in 6 mm came; panels cemented, and the lancet returned behind new isothermal protective glazing.

COMMANDE · THE LITURGY OF A WINDOW

Five stations,
sketch to stone

A commissioned window passes through the same five stations it has since the workshop opened. The order is fixed; the glass is not.

  1. I

    AUDIENCE

    We visit the building, measure the openings, and sit in the room at three hours of the day to learn where its light comes from and what it lands on. You tell us what the window is for.

    1 visit · no charge within Champagne
  2. II

    CARTON

    A full-size cartoon in charcoal and wash, drawn at the atelier and pinned against daylight for your approval. The cutline — every lead the window will ever have — is fixed here.

    3–6 weeks
  3. III

    COUPE

    Glass is pulled from the rack and held to the sky, sheet by sheet, before it is cut. A colour that sings on the bench can go mute at forty feet; we choose at distance, not at arm's length.

    2–4 weeks
  4. IV

    CUISSON

    Grisaille shadows, silver-stain golds, then three firings at 620 °C to make the painting part of the glass itself. Nothing applied cold ever leaves this workshop.

    2–3 weeks · 1953 Guérin kiln
  5. V

    POSE

    Leading, cementing, and installation by our own hands, with isothermal glazing where the stone allows. We return after the first winter to check the cement, at our own charge.

    1–2 weeks on site

VERRERIE · THE GLASS RACK

Twelve glasses,
held to the light

Every sheet in the rack is mouth-blown for us by three furnaces we have used for generations. T is transmission — how much of the sky each colour lets through. Rest on a pane to hold it up to the light.
